How to stop clenching your jaw: what actually works

There's no single switch — but there is a proven stack: awareness training, the right device for the right time of day, and unloading the muscles while the habit retrains.

LG
Medically reviewed by our GDC-registered clinical teamWritten by Un-clench · Updated July 2026

First: day clenching and night clenching are different problems

The single most useful thing you can learn about clenching is that the awake habit and the sleep habit have different drivers and different fixes. Awake clenching is a stress-and-concentration behaviour — it responds to awareness training. Sleep clenching and grinding happens below consciousness — you can't train it away directly, so the goal is protection and reducing the inputs that drive it. Most persistent clenchers do both, which is why half-solutions fail: a night guard alone leaves you clenching all day; willpower alone does nothing at 3am.

Stopping the daytime clench

The best-evidenced approach for awake clenching is habit reversal: repeatedly noticing the clench and consciously releasing to the correct rest position — lips together, teeth apart, tongue resting gently on the palate. It works, but only with enough repetitions, which is why prompts matter: recurring phone reminders, a note on your monitor, or a structured programme of nudges. Expect two to three weeks of deliberate practice before the new default starts holding on its own.

A thin daytime plate accelerates this. Worn at your desk, it does two jobs: it protects the teeth in the moment, and every time you bite into it you get instant feedback — you notice, you release. That feedback loop is precisely what retrains the habit.

Protecting the night

For sleep clenching, a custom-fitted night guard is the standard of care — not to stop the grinding, but to take the force, protect enamel and dental work, and ease the morning jaw ache and temple headaches. Fit and build matter: loose boil-and-bite guards can make clenching worse, and the right hardness depends on how heavily you grind. Alongside the guard, reduce the evening inputs with the strongest evidence of aggravating sleep bruxism: late caffeine, alcohol, and screens close to bedtime.

Unloading the muscles while you retrain

While the habit work happens, the muscles that have been overworking — the masseter at the jaw angle and the temporalis at the temples — respond well to direct care: warmth, massage, and trigger-point release. A few minutes of deliberate work at the end of the day genuinely eases the ache, and paired with magnesium's established role in normal muscle function, it makes the retraining period far more comfortable.

When to escalate

If you've done all of the above and the tension, headaches or jaw bulk persist — or you're a heavy, long-term clencher — the muscle itself can be treated at source with prescription muscle-relaxing treatment, assessed and delivered in-clinic. And some symptoms should skip self-help entirely: jaw locking, new or worsening headaches, or signs of a sleep-breathing problem all need clinical assessment first.
